Abstract

Resonance appears in natural and engineered systems including acoustics, structures, radio circuits, and mechanical vibrations. This paper discusses resonance in violins using Benade's and Hutchins' resonance curves, resonance in bridges with focus on the Tacoma Narrows collapse, and resonance in radio LC circuits used in communication devices. Understanding resonance allows engineers to prevent structural failure and optimise device performance. Reference source

Violins

The acoustics of each unique violin is determined through its frequencies and amplitudes of the resonance, producing a rich and full sound. The vibrating strings transfer their energy to the violins wooden body, which resonates inside its wooden chamber. It amplifies and sustains the sound allowing world-class musicians to fill orchestral halls like the Royal Albert Hall. A larger body like the main framed will produce a deeper warmer sound with a more powerful bass response because it can more efficiently resonate with the longer wavelengths of lower pitches. A smaller internal volume (smaller body size like the scroll) increases the resonance frequency, resulting in a brighter tone with less depth. Reference source

Bridges

Resonance in bridges occurs when an external force (such as wind) matches the bridge’s natural frequency, causing large oscillations.

The 1940 Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed due to aeroelastic flutter—a self-exciting vibration where the movement of the bridge generated alternating forces that increased its amplitude.

Modern bridges use dampers and segmented designs to interrupt resonant waves and prevent destructive oscillations. Reference source

Radios

Radio receivers use a resonant LC circuit (inductor + capacitor) tuned to a specific frequency. At resonance, oscillations reach maximum amplitude, allowing the radio to select one broadcasting station while rejecting others.

This principle is used in remote controllers, drones, Wi-Fi systems, and mobile phones, where resonant circuits filter desired frequencies for clear communication. Reference source
