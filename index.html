Bridges

    Resonance in Bridges occurs when the bridge’s natural frequency is the same as an external force like wind. This causes the bridge’s vibrations to increase and grow dramatically in amplitude, resulting in dangerous swinging and in some cases destruction.
    In 1940 the ‘Tacoma Narrows Bridge’ collapsed, destroyed by wind as in had a torsional natural frequency of approximately 0.2Hz. The winds vortex was calculated around 1Hz, much higher than the bridge’s, but a phenomenon known as aeroelastic flutter where the bridge’s motion itself generated alternating forces that grew in amplitude, developing forces at a frequency matching 0.2Hz.
    In order to stop resonance occurring and help maintain a bridge that withstands wind and other factors, engineers incorporate dampeners into the bridge interrupting the resonant waves to prevent them from growing in amplitude causing destruction.
    Another design to halt resonance is the construct different sections with overlapping plates to make it difficult for the resonant waves to travel along. The movement of one section to another causes friction, changing the frequency preventing the bridge’s wave being similar to the natural frequence resulting in zero resonance.website on violins and shit
